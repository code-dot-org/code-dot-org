Lesson 3: v1 Unit 2 Lesson 3 Invent an Addressing Protocol

Unplugged | Group Problem Solving | Widget | Internet Simulator

Overview

Building off the need for addressing messages that arose during the Battleship game activity, this lesson asks students to take time to invent a communication protocol and describe its binary encoding. The challenge is to make the protocol efficient (in terms of number of bits required) and flexible (able to handle different situations). Students discuss the requirements of an addressing protocol - minimally, the sender and recipient’s identifying information - perhaps including the size of the message, date sent, priority, etc. Students choose a number of bits for addresses and justify this choice with the maximum number of users this number of bits could support. Students also discuss why IP addresses must be of a fixed length.

Purpose

In this lesson students will invent two ideas central to the Internet and how it works: fixed-size numeric addresses for computers (IP addresses) and a structured “packet” that standardizes the bit layout for information you need to send. Students will invent an addressing scheme and message (datagram) structure for playing Battleship, but these same ideas form a communication protocol that is very similar to real IP addresses and IPv4 packets that are used on the Internet.

Teaching Guide

Getting Started

Transition:

  • In the last lesson, you and your partner(s) had to figure out how to identify yourselves in your Battleship messages. Each message had to include information about who sent it and where it was going. We call that “addressing information,” for obvious reasons! It’s the same with sending a letter through the mail: you need your recipient’s address and the return address on it.

How are people identified on a phone network?

By their phone number, duh.

  • What are the benefits of using a phone number? Think about the characteristics of phone numbers. Why was the phone system designed to assign a number to every phone instead of a name? How many total phone numbers are there?
    • There are about 10 billion phone numbers, 999-999-9999 phone numbers for the United States (more world wide if you include international codes).
    • There is a fixed number of digits. This is important for predictability and usefulness; if phone numbers varied in length it would be tough to track.
    • Names vary in length too much and there are many many duplicates, no unique identifier. Phone numbers allow a unique ID to be attached to any device.
  • Why do you think phone numbers have 10 digits? Why not 9 or 11 or 15?
    • Someone thought that 10 billion numbers would be enough. Given that there are about 320 million people (including children and babies) in the United States, it would be enough for each person to have 25 or 30 phone numbers.

Activity

Activity: Invent a binary addressing protocol for Battleship.

Hand out and go over Activity Guide - Inventing a Protocol for Battleship.

Teaching Tips

Lead students with these questions if they are having difficulty coming up with protocols:

  • How will you standardize the recipient and sender addresses?
  • Where in the message will you place your addresses? Recipient or sender first?
  • How do you know where one ends and the other begins?
  • What other information do you need to include?

Students might come up with ways to encode their names in binary, but they might not see the benefits of assigning numbers to people instead. Challenge students with these questions. Prompt:

  • If students simply encoded their names into binary:
    • How many people in the world have the same name? Is it a good idea to map a sender or recipient’s identity to their real name? What if their name is John Smith?
  • If students used full names or additional identifying information:
    • Should there be a limit on the length of your protocol? What is the downside of using long names?
  • If students did not use fixed-length addressing:
    • How does the Internet know to separate the addressing information from the message itself? That is, how does the Internet know where the protocol part ends and the message begins?

Share: Students should present their protocols. Prompt: What are some common elements of the protocols your classmates came up with? Are there any problems?

Discuss:

  • You can’t use real names as addresses on the Internet. More than one person can share the same name. Sender and recipient information needs to be unique.
  • You can’t just translate a person’s name and info into binary, either. This takes up way too much binary space! Addresses are probably numeric in nature.
  • We need a clear way for the Internet to know when the protocol ends and the message begins, and we only have 0s and 1s to work with.

Explain:

  • A good solution uses a numeric address (not tied to a person’s name) of fixed length. The numeric part deals with the concerns of a needing a unique identifier and taking up less space, and the fixed length solves the problem of letting the Internet know where the message begins. If all addresses are the same length, it’s easy to define where addresses start and end.
  • How many bits do you need for addresses?
    • For 3-person Battleship, you only need 2 bits for an address, since there are only 3 people; each can have a unique code.
  • How many bits are needed for the total message?
    • Answers to this will vary and creativity should be encouraged. However, it should be evident from the students’ protocols that they recognize the fundamental elements of the game and what might be able to be compressed into only a few bits. For example, in the game board the columns are given as letters A, B, C; since there are only 3 possibilities, the column could be encoded with 2 bits rather than, say using the full 8-bit ASCII code.

Exemplar:

A simple, but clever encoding protocol is to recognize that the addresses, coordinates, and hit/miss messages can each be encoded with 2 bits (i.e. a list of 5 numbers in the range 0-3), and that you can make a combined message protocol that would allow a player to communicate anything needed for the game.

The scheme shown below uses 10 bits total. The last two bits are interesting: they indicate whether this is a shot being fired for the given row and column or whether it’s a hit/miss response to the given row and column. Since the respondent who says hit or miss must verify the coordinates of the shot they are responding to, the recipient doesn’t have to remember the last shot they called.

Communication protocols are most useful and less error prone when a message contains everything the recipient needs to know to process the message.

Wrap-up

Recap IP address background and terminology:

Teacher can give this info in interest of time; a suggested visual aid: show a real IPv4 packet.

  • The message structure you invented for your Battleship protocol is generally described as a “datagram” or “packet”.
  • The real Internet uses a protocol called…wait for it…the “Internet Protocol” or IP. Computers have numeric addresses called “IP addresses”.
  • The most common datagram or packet structure in use today is called an “IPv4 packet” (which means IP version 4).
  • The Internet Protocol is a general protocol used to send messages of any type.

So we learned about real IP addresses and real IP packets. Tomorrow we’ll look at our Internet Simulator version of packets, which is greatly simplified.

Test Your Protocol in the Internet Simulator (Optional): Students have been provided access to the Internet Simulator. If they have time students may wish to test their protocols there. While interpreting the binary information is not explicitly the goal of this lesson, it will provide an opportunity to think more critically about what information they have included in their protocols.

Extended Learning

  • How else is the Internet Simulator different right now from how you think the Internet really works?
  • An interesting question to ask is: what would you change about the way the Internet Simulator works right now if you could? Some possibilities:
    • You still have to view everyone’s messages to everyone and pick out the ones meant for you. That’s a lot of noise, and a lot of messages to sift through! It would be nice if only the messages meant for you were sent directly to you.
    • It’s difficult to remember everyone’s numeric IP address. It would be nice if you could assign nicknames or some other shortcut.
    • If you forget someone’s IP address, there’s no way to look it up. It would be nice if there were some equivalent of a phone book, so you could find out your friends’ IP addresses. (Keep in mind, current technology like email does not work this way – you can’t just look up a friend’s email address unless they tell you what it is.)
    • There’s no security! Anyone can look at anyone’s messages, or pretend to be anybody else! It would be nice if there was some protection of Internet communication.
  • Blown to Bits (www.bitsbook.com), Appendix - The Internet as System and Spirit, pp. 301-309 (The Internet as a Communication System), then answer the following questions:
    • The layers of protocols used in network communication is an example of abstraction. Can you give other examples of abstraction in everyday life?
    • Compare and contrast sending a postcard versus sending an email.
