Lesson 4: v1 Unit 2 Lesson 10 DNS in the Real World

Overview

Baker Note: this lesson is being merged into the previous one “the Need for DNS”

In this lesson, students identify the similarities between DNS and the postal service, focusing on their hierarchical structure. They then explore stories from the news about DNS and some of the vulnerabilities of the system. Students perform a jigsaw research activity by reading one article in a small group then changing groups and sharing their new knowledge with other students in the class. The articles should serve to help students begin thinking about the global impact of different protocols on the Internet by bringing current events to the classroom.

Purpose

DNS (Domain Name Server) is a hierarchical system that translates domain names into IP addresses. At the top level of DNS are the root servers, which contain information for domains for such as .com or .org. By accessing the correct root, and looking at each of its branches, it is possible for the DNS to follow the branches down to second level and subdomains to find the appropriate IP address. A single DNS may not know all of the information necessary to find an IP address, but by asking other DNSes, it is able to find that information.

The DNS is an open standard protocol, which makes it vulnerable to security issues. These issues include DNS cache poisoning and DDoS attacks, among others. In DNS cache poisoning, DNS servers are given an incorrect IP address, thus routing anyone looking for that information to an incorrect website. DDoS attacks occur when a DNS server is sent too many requests at once and is unable to field all of them. This results in the DNS being unable to serve any request, essentially blocking the DNS from use.

Teaching Guide

Getting Started

Goal: The DNS is both redundant and hierarchical in nature. Students will watch a video to help better understand its hierarchy, then discuss the analogy between the DNS and the postal service.

Video: How DNS Works.

Video: Show students the video How DNS Works.

Discussion.

Say: While we can’t actually see what’s going on in the DNS in real life, we can compare it to a system that you’ve seen before: the postal service. (See Code Studio levels 2.)

  • How can you relate DNS and the postal service? Describe their analogous hierarchical structure.
    • Scaling of DNS requires system of servers organized in a hierarchy, just like there is a system of post offices and hierarchy (main city post office and local neighborhood post offices). Hierarchy of DNS/IP is analogous to country/state/city/zip/street/direction/number. New URL/IP address pairs are sometimes added, just like new homes and addresses are added and the post office needs to know. IP addresses for web pages sometimes do change, just like people sometimes move. When you move, you send in a change of address card; when a web page’s IP address changes, it needs to update its DNS, which then shares the IP change with other DNS servers.
  • How do DNS and the postal service function differently?
    • If you send mail to someone who has moved, it sometimes comes back undelivered. The Internet cannot have undelivered messages, so the DNS server is queried for the current IP address for EVERY web page you visit.

Transition: To learn more about DNS you will be reading articles about how it works and some of the vulnerabilities of the system, namely DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) attacks. You will have 20 minutes to read, then we will perform a jigsaw and you will learn about the other articles from your classmates.

Activity

Introduction: Students will need to be placed in groups of 4-6 people to complete their readings. Distribute Activity Guide - Research: DNS in the Real World, one copy per student. Assign each group one of the articles (they can be found on Code Studio as well as below in the lesson plan) and ask that they complete the first page of the sheet. Encourage groups to work together and make sure that at the end of the reading time, all students in the group feel comfortable with the technical content in the article. If groups are finished early, suggest that they do more research on the subject of their article and DDoS attacks.

Research with DNS articles.

Provide students 15-20 minutes to read their article and make sense of the content. Groups should work together to complete their activity guide.

Teaching Tips

Some articles are shorter than others and may not take the full 15 minutes to read. Consider giving these to students with lower reading levels or pairing them together so that a group reads two articles.

Jigsaw: Number students within their groups, then send students to create new physical groups depending upon their new number (e.g., all the students labeled “1” move to create a group in the same part of the room). Students should exchange information they learned with one another, recording the key points from each article in the space provided on the second page of the activity guide.

Wrap-up

Discussion: Conclude the lesson with a sense-making discussion summarizing the broad themes of the day. The two key points are how DNS functions in the real world and DDoS attacks. If students are having a hard time visualizing DDoS attacks, consider using this infographic: Anatomy of DNS DDoS Attack. Prompts:

  • What makes DNS hierarchical?
  • What is DDoS? What makes DNS susceptible to it?
    • Distributed Denial of Service occurs when a DNS receives too many requests at once. This causes the DNS to be “blocked.” DNS is susceptible because it is an open public communication protocol, which means anyone has access to it.
  • What are other disadvantages of the open standard of addressing that the Internet uses?
    • DNS can be altered by governments or organizations to prevent access to certain locations on the Internet. (If you don’t know the IP, you can’t send or receive traffic.)
    • Manipulating the DNS can allow an attacker to read traffic coming from your computer and even impersonate you on the Internet. Traffic intended for you can instead be rerouted to an attacker
