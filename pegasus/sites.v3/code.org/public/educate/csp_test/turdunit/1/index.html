Lesson 1: v1 Unit 1 Lesson 3 Sending Complex Messages

Overview

Baker note 4.4.16: moving content from this lesson into previous lesson.

In a continuation of the previous lesson, students are asked to consider how to use their binary messaging devices to send a more complex message - a message with more than two possibilities - by modifying how they use their device, rather than by modifying the device itself. The result is that students have to invent systems of communication that use combinations of binary signals. This lesson will also introduce the term “bit” as another way to describe a binary message. This lays the foundations for a) encoding complex information in computers using combinations of bits as well as b) developing a communication protocol for transmitting those bits from one place to another.

Purpose

There are two major connections that should be made in this lesson. The first connection to make is that even though everyone’s binary message-sending devices are different, since they all are designed to send a binary message, all the devices can achieve two states, which we call state A and state B for the time being. It also means that any one of them can be used to send any kind of message, as long as the messages are binary. The second major connection to make is that in order to send messages a communication protocol must be established. That means there must be an agreement between the sender and receiver about how messages will be formed, communicated, and interpreted.

Teaching Guide

Getting Started

Goal: The goal of this discussion is to draw out the limitations of simply trying to modify the device to make it able to represent more and more states. We want to motivate the idea that, rather than changing the device, we can invent a new way to use it such that each possible message maps to a unique sequence of bits communicated by the device.

Introduction

Remarks

As we discussed in the previous class, information is a fairly abstract concept. To send information we need to encode it in some way, or in other words turn it into something physical that the receiver could recognize. The information we sent with our devices was binary because it could only take one of two states. We call this type of information a bit, which is short for “binary digit.” This means that yesterday you were using your devices to encode bits. This was pretty convenient, since the messages we were sending were also binary.

Think, Pair, Share

Remarks

Imagine that you now have to communicate an answer to a question that has 16 possible responses, instead of 2. How would you modify the device or the way you use it in order to answer a question that has a larger set of possible responses?

  • Students journal or think quietly before discussing their ideas with their teammates.
  • Do a quick whip-around to get students’ ideas out; you may want to write them on the board.

It is possible that students will suggest modifying their device so that it can represent 16 different states instead of 2. If that happens, prompt students to consider:

  • What if it wasn’t 16 possible messages that you wanted to send, but 100, or 1,000 or 1,000,000? Is it really feasible to keep modifying your device to represent more possible states?
  • Rather than modifying the device, can you modify the way you use it to communicate more messages?

Transition

Yesterday we sent bits. You just considered sending messages that could take more than two states, i.e. are no longer bits. As our messages grow in complexity, however, it seems that the strategy of letting our messages take more than two states will quickly become unmanageable. For today’s challenge, you will need to devise a scheme that allows you to send a message that can take more than two states, while the information in the message itself is composed of only bits.

Activity

Devise a scenario with more than two possible messages that need to be communicated.

Goal: Students recognize that the bit-sending device they designed yesterday can be used to send messages more complex than a binary message, even if the components of the message are still bits. Students recognize the need for agreeing upon a protocol for interpreting sequences of bits.

Complex message challenge: Distribute Activity Guide - Sending Complex Messages.

Provide students a question that has four possible answers, along with the potential answers.

  • Favorite Season? Winter, Spring, Summer, Fall
  • Direction towards your house? North, South, East and West
  • What time zone is [insert a location here] in? Pacific, Mountain, Central, Eastern
  • Feel free to design your own!

Devise a way to communicate those messages using your bit-sending device.

Challenge Guidelines: Say: Your task is to invent a way to communicate an answer to the question using the agreed-upon answers without modifying your device.

  • Use the same representations of states as in the previous lesson (e.g.: pulling a string one time for state A and pulling the string two times for state B).
  • You can only send one bit (symbol/state) at a time.
  • Each of the 4 messages must be represented by a sequence or combination of bits.

Teaching Tips

Testing and optimizing messaging system

  • As groups are testing their systems, they should be encouraged to improve their design to make it more efficient, or faster, or more resistant to failure.
  • You might motivate students by saying, for example, imagine that it costs $100 to send each bit across the system. How can you minimize the cost of sending the message, regardless of which message is required to answer the question?
    • Note: The optimal solution is to use only 2 bits. 2 bits have 4 different state-combinations (AA, AB, BA, BB). A bit is simply a binary message, so a sequence of bits is an organized set of binary messages, where the order in which the symbols are sent matters.

Share communication strategies with the group.

Sharing Solutions: The following activity can be used both to share student solutions and as an assessment of the protocol students design. For other ideas on how to conclude this activity check out the learning strategies.

The teacher prepares four notecards with the possible responses to the question written on them. One at a time, groups come to the front of the class. The teacher randomly chooses one card and shows it only to the sending members of the group. The sending members then use their protocol to send that message. The receiving team indicates what message they received and compares results with the intended message. Members of the audience can ask questions about the protocol or provide helpful feedback on how to improve it.

  • Note: While students are still sending the responses to the questions, because these are the four possible messages that they can send, the teacher is no longer asking the question of the group. Instead, the teacher is making a decision about which of the four responses should be sent.

Wrap-up

Class Discussion: Provide the class an opportunity to synthesize ideas and experiences from the activity. Key ideas for review include the ability to combine bits to send more complex messages, and the need for clear protocols to interpret sequences of bits. Prompt:

  • Could you use another group’s device to send your set of messages? Why or why not?
    • Are their protocols clear? Do the devices work?
  • Were some protocols more efficient than others? How would you measure efficiency?
    • Note: Ask students to analyze which device(s) require the fewest bits in all instances.

Rubric: Share Rubric - Sending Complex Messages for students to self-assess the work of the group.

Assessment

Rubric: Assess student achievement with Rubric - Sending Complex Messages.

Additional Questions:

  • Can you send a message in binary to someone you’ve never before communicated with? If yes, how? If no, what does the person receiving a message need to know in order to successfully decode the message?
  • Why did you choose your particular message encoding strategy? List at least two reasons that justify your decision. List two issues or problems that could be improved in your encoding strategy.

Extended Learning

  • Ask students to create a user manual for their device, or make a video demonstrating its use.
  • Instruct students to record their thoughts about the devices created by the class. Prompt: Did the devices surprise you? How is your device different from the devices created by others? How is it similar?
